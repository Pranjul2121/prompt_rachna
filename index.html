Garuda Purana · A Tale of Destiny

Garuda Purana · Prarabdha Karma

Garuda's
Compassion

A parrot. A god's startled gaze.
And the wings that, in mercy, carried fate to its appointed place.

I  ·  Mount Kailash
Scene One  ·  The Waiting Eagle
The gate of Kailash
"Even the greatest serve patiently
at the threshold of the divine."

On a morning that shone like polished silver, Lord Vishnu — Preserver of the Universe, blue as the deep sky after rain — rose from his cosmic rest and set out for Mount Kailash, the silver throne of Lord Shiva.

His beloved vehicle and devoted servant, Garuda — the great eagle-god, king of all birds, whose wings could scatter storm clouds — waited respectfully outside the heavenly gate while the two great lords met inside.

Garuda sat on a stone pillar, his golden wings folded, his sharp amber eyes wandering across the mountainside. The air at Kailash was different — cool, thin, luminous, as though the sky itself held its breath.

Then he noticed it: perched on a branch of a small tree near the gate, a tiny parrot. Emerald-green, red-chinned, perfectly still. It sang a quiet note to itself. It preened one wing. It blinked at Garuda with the casual confidence of a creature that does not yet know the word danger.

Garuda smiled. Even in the court of gods, small beautiful things found their way.

II  ·  The Gaze of Yama
Scene Two  ·  The Startled God
The Lord of Death
"It was not the look of a hunter spotting prey.
It was the look of a man who cannot believe his own ledger."

After some time, the heavy air shifted. The mountain's birds fell silent. The gate's torches bent without wind.

Yamraj — God of Death, Keeper of Dharma, dark-crowned and riding his great buffalo — arrived at Kailash for his own appointment with Lord Shiva. He walked with the unhurried calm of one for whom time is not a river but a ledger.

As he passed through the gate, something made him stop.

He turned. His eyes fell on the small parrot on the branch. And then — something extraordinary. The God of Death, who has witnessed the end of ten thousand universes, who does not blink at kings or mountains, looked at this tiny bird with an expression that Garuda could only describe as pure surprise. His brow furrowed. He shook his head slowly, as a man does when confronted with an impossible puzzle, and walked inside.

The whole encounter had lasted four seconds.

Garuda's heart tightened. In his long life, he had never seen Yama surprised. Yama did not do surprise. Yama knew. Yama always knew.

"Something is wrong," Garuda thought. "That look could only mean one thing — this little parrot's time is very nearly up."

III  ·  The Act of Mercy
Scene Three  ·  Wings of Compassion
Mercy at the speed of storm
"He acted the way the truly compassionate act —
immediately, without hesitation, without asking whether he could."

Garuda did not deliberate. Compassion in the great do not wait for reason. He stepped down from the pillar, walked to the little tree, and cupped the parrot gently in his enormous golden hands. The bird looked up at him with bright, unafraid eyes.

Then Garuda spread his wings.

The wingspan of Garuda is said to stretch from one horizon to another. When he flies, entire weather systems move aside. In a single beat of his wings, he crossed mountain ranges. In three beats, he crossed oceans. The clouds below him turned into a white carpet and then disappeared.

He was searching in his mind for the safest place in all creation — somewhere Death could not easily reach. Far. Isolated. Hidden. He thought of a deep forest he had once flown over, in a land thousands of miles from Kailash, where the trees grew so thick that sunlight itself arrived in pieces.

There, in a cliff face at the forest's edge, he found what he was looking for: a small crevice in the rock, dark and deep, invisible to any eye above. He placed the parrot inside with infinite care, nestled between two dry stones.

"Stay here, little one. You are safe. No predator will find you. No eye will reach you here."

He stepped back and looked at the hidden crevice. Satisfied. Certain. The quiet pride of someone who has done a good thing.

Then Garuda turned and flew back to Kailash.

IV  ·  The Question
Scene Four  ·  Garuda Asks
The conversation at the gate
"He had saved the parrot. Now he wanted to understand
what he had saved it from."

When Yamraj emerged from his meeting and walked back through the gate, Garuda was waiting. He bowed respectfully — even the great bow before the just — and asked his question with careful humility:

"Lord Yamraj, forgive my curiosity. When you arrived this morning, you stopped and looked at the small parrot near the gate. You seemed… surprised. I have never seen you surprised. May I ask — what did you see?"

Yamraj paused. He looked at Garuda with those ancient, measuring eyes. Then he answered, calmly, the way a man answers when the answer is simply the truth and requires no decoration:

"I was surprised because, according to the records of destiny I carry, that parrot was fated to die this very hour — eaten by a python, in a forest crevice, thousands of miles from here. When I saw the bird sitting calmly at Kailash, I could not understand how it would travel that distance in time. I shook my head because the numbers did not add up."

Yamraj looked at Garuda. A long moment.

"Now I understand."

V  ·  The Revelation
Scene Five  ·  The Unbearable Truth
Fate's appointed room
"It was not cruelty. It was not a mistake.
It was the shape of what was always going to happen."

Garuda went very still.

The forest crevice. The dark cleft between two stones. The place he had chosen because it seemed hidden, safe, invisible.

The python had been there first.

In trying to save the parrot from death, Garuda had become the very instrument of its fate. His wings — those magnificent, storm-scattering wings that could cross oceans in three beats — had carried the small bird across thousands of miles and placed it, with infinite tenderness, into the exact crevice where the snake was waiting. Death had not chased the parrot. Garuda had delivered it.

"You carried it there yourself," said Yamraj, without cruelty, without satisfaction. The way a physician states a diagnosis. "In your compassion, you fulfilled the very destiny you sought to prevent. The record was always correct. Only the method was unknown — until now."

Yamraj nodded once, as if closing a completed account, and walked on into the eternal road that passes through all worlds.

Garuda stood at the gate of Kailash for a long time, his great wings folded, looking at the small tree where the parrot had been sitting, which was now empty, as it was always going to be.

VI  ·  What Garuda Understood
Scene Six  ·  The Still Eagle
After knowledge
"He had not saved the parrot.
He had loved it. That, perhaps,
was not nothing."

There is a question the Garuda Purana quietly asks after this story: Was Garuda wrong to try?

The sages who retell this story do not say he was. Garuda acted from pure compassion. He saw a creature in potential danger and he moved — immediately, powerfully, selflessly. That the outcome was woven before he ever lifted a feather does not make his impulse false. It makes it human. Divine, even, in its helplessness.

What the story teaches is not: do not try.

What it teaches is this: effort belongs to us. Outcome belongs to karma. We act with full sincerity and full love — and we release the result. The archer draws the bow with all their skill. Where the arrow finally lands is not the archer's kingdom.

Prarabdha karma — the karma already set in motion, already in flight — cannot be recalled. Not by the love of eagles. Not by the compassion of gods. The wheel turns where it was always turning.

Garuda returned to his pillar outside the gate and waited for Lord Vishnu, as he always did. But from that morning, those who knew him said there was something different in the way he held his wings — a certain lightness, as if he had set down a great weight he had not known he was carrying. The weight of believing that love, if large enough, could rewrite what is written.

Perhaps that is the real mercy the story offers — not a way to escape fate, but a way to make peace with it.

The Teaching · Garuda Purana
Destiny cannot be outflown. Not even by Garuda's wings.   Act with love. Act with full force. Then let the outcome go.

Prarabdha Karma — the portion of karma already in motion — cannot be altered by action, however well-intentioned. What we control is the sincerity of our effort and the purity of our motive. What we do not control is the final shape of things.

Sometimes, running from fate carries us toward it. The only freedom is not in the outcome, but in the quality of presence we bring to each moment before the outcome arrives.
